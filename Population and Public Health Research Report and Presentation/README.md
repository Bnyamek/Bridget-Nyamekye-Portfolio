## Project Overview

**Title:** *The Burden and Outcomes of Dengue-Related Hospitalizations in the United States*

This is an exploratory research project investigating dengue fever as an **emerging public health threat in the United States**. While dengue is extensively documented globally, its impact on the U.S. healthcare system remains significantly understudied. This project synthesizes existing literature to characterize the burden of dengue-related hospitalizations, identify high-risk populations, and highlight critical gaps in national surveillance and data infrastructure.

The research draws on epidemiological literature, global burden-of-disease studies, U.S. administrative healthcare data (HCUP), and CDC/WHO surveillance reports to build a comprehensive picture of dengue's current and projected impact on the United States.

---

## Research Question

> **"What is the burden and outcomes of dengue-related hospitalizations in the United States, and what gaps exist in understanding its impact on high-risk populations and the U.S. healthcare system?"**

This question was motivated by the observation that — despite dengue being one of the most prevalent mosquito-borne viral diseases globally — **comprehensive, U.S.-specific studies on hospitalization outcomes, comorbidity profiles, and healthcare utilization are largely absent from the literature**.

---

## Background & Significance

Dengue fever is a mosquito-borne viral disease caused by the **dengue virus (DENV)**, transmitted primarily by *Aedes* mosquitoes (*Aedes aegypti* and *Aedes albopictus*).

**Global burden:**
- Approximately **390 million dengue infections** occur annually worldwide (Bhatt et al., 2013)
- **5–10% of infections** progress to severe dengue, which can lead to significant morbidity and mortality (WHO, 2024)
- The global economic burden is substantial, driven by hospitalization costs, vector control, and productivity loss (Shepard et al., 2016)

**Why the United States matters:**
Dengue is increasingly recognized as an **emerging concern in the U.S.** due to:
- Rising international travel connecting the U.S. to endemic regions
- **Climate change** expanding the geographic range of *Aedes* mosquito vectors into previously non-endemic areas
- Regions such as **Florida, Texas, and Puerto Rico** facing particularly elevated risk due to favorable environmental conditions

Despite this growing threat, the burden and outcomes of dengue-related hospitalizations in the U.S. remain **poorly characterized**, particularly among vulnerable groups such as older adults and individuals with chronic comorbidities.

## Methodology

This is an **exploratory/scoping research project** based on a structured literature review and synthesis of existing epidemiological and health services data. The methodology involved:

**1. Literature Review**
- Reviewed peer-reviewed studies on dengue epidemiology, global burden, pathogenesis, and hospitalization outcomes
- Sources included *Nature*, *The Lancet*, *The Lancet Infectious Diseases*, *PLoS Neglected Tropical Diseases*, and *Emerging Infectious Diseases*

**2. Epidemiological Data Sources**
- **WHO (2024)** global dengue surveillance reports
- **CDC (2024)** dengue data specific to the United States
- **AHRQ Healthcare Cost and Utilization Project (HCUP, 2024)** — administrative inpatient data used to examine hospitalization patterns

**3. ICD-10 Diagnostic Code Analysis**
- Examined the use of ICD-10 codes in administrative datasets to identify dengue-related hospitalizations
- Critically assessed limitations including coding inaccuracies, misclassification, and their effects on epidemiological validity

**4. High-Risk Population Identification**
- Focused analysis on populations at elevated risk for adverse dengue outcomes:
  - **Older adults**
  - **Individuals with chronic comorbidities** (e.g., diabetes, cardiovascular disease)
  - Residents of **endemic U.S. regions** (Florida, Texas, Puerto Rico)

**5. Gap Analysis**
- Compared the depth of U.S.-specific dengue research against global literature to identify critical evidence gaps in healthcare utilization, inpatient outcomes, and surveillance infrastructure

## Key Findings

### Public Health Impact
- Dengue contributes substantially to **morbidity and healthcare utilization**, particularly in endemic U.S. regions
- Outbreaks generate **considerable economic costs** through hospitalization expenses, vector control programs, and workforce productivity loss
- **Vulnerable populations** — including older adults and individuals with chronic diseases — face disproportionately higher risk for adverse dengue outcomes (Guzman and Harris, 2015)

### Evidence Gap in the United States
- Comprehensive studies examining **dengue hospitalization outcomes at a national U.S. level are largely absent** from the literature
- Existing research does not adequately address:
  - Healthcare utilization patterns
  - Comorbidity profiles of hospitalized dengue patients
  - Regional variation in inpatient outcomes across U.S. states
- Most available research is concentrated on **endemic countries** in Southeast Asia and Latin America, leaving the U.S. healthcare system underexamined

### Surveillance Limitations
- **Underreporting and misdiagnosis** are common due to symptom overlap with other febrile illnesses such as influenza, Zika, and chikungunya (Roy and Bhattacharjee, 2021)
- **ICD-10 coding inaccuracies** in administrative data introduce misclassification bias, potentially leading to underestimates of true disease burden
- Gaps in **real-time surveillance systems** and data integration limit the ability of public health authorities to monitor and respond to outbreaks promptly

### Climate & Emerging Transmission Risk
- Climate change and **environmental unpredictability** are expanding *Aedes* mosquito habitats into new U.S. regions, complicating dengue transmission forecasting (Messina et al., 2019)
- Local transmission events (e.g., Radke et al., 2012 — Key West, Florida) demonstrate that dengue emergence within the continental U.S. is not hypothetical but actively occurring

## Challenges in Dengue Research

| Challenge | Description |
|---|---|
| **Underreporting & Misdiagnosis** | Symptom overlap with other febrile illnesses leads to missed or incorrect diagnoses |
| **Limited U.S.-Specific Data** | Most dengue hospitalization research focuses on endemic countries, not the U.S. |
| **ICD-10 Coding Inaccuracies** | Administrative data misclassification affects the validity of epidemiological estimates |
| **Climate Unpredictability** | Expanding mosquito vectors into new regions makes transmission modeling difficult |
| **Surveillance Gaps** | Fragmented surveillance infrastructure hinders real-time outbreak monitoring and response |

## Related Work

| Study | Key Contribution |
|---|---|
| Bhatt et al. (2013) | Comprehensive estimate of global dengue incidence (~390M/year); highlighted significant underreporting |
| Stanaway et al. (2016) | Quantified global dengue burden using DALYs; showed substantial increase in disease impact over time |
| Radke et al. (2012) | Documented local dengue transmission in Key West, Florida — evidence of U.S. emergence |
| Roy & Bhattacharjee (2021) | Explored biological mechanisms underlying dengue disease progression and severity |
| Messina et al. (2019) | Modeled current and future global distribution of dengue under climate change scenarios |
| Guzman & Harris (2015) | Characterized increased risk of adverse outcomes in older adults and those with comorbidities |
| Shepard et al. (2016) | Quantified the global economic burden of dengue including hospitalization and productivity costs |

**Critical gap identified:** None of the above studies provide a comprehensive national-level analysis of dengue hospitalization outcomes, comorbidity profiles, or healthcare utilization patterns specifically within the **United States healthcare system**.

## Opportunities for Future Research

1. **Enhanced Surveillance Infrastructure**
   - Improve data integration across federal, state, and local public health systems
   - Develop real-time dengue monitoring dashboards leveraging **machine learning and predictive analytics** for early outbreak detection and risk stratification

2. **Climate-Based Epidemiological Modeling**
   - Apply climate-based models to predict the geographic expansion of dengue transmission risk across the U.S. (Ryan et al., 2019)
   - Integrate environmental data with hospitalization records to understand regional vulnerability

3. **U.S.-Focused Hospitalization Studies**
   - Conduct national-level analyses using HCUP data to characterize:
     - Hospitalization rates and lengths of stay
     - Comorbidity burden among dengue inpatients
     - Regional and demographic disparities in outcomes

4. **Vaccine Effectiveness Research**
   - Evaluate dengue vaccine efficacy and safety in non-endemic U.S. populations
   - Study therapeutic intervention outcomes as dengue transmission expands into new U.S. regions

---

## Conclusion

Dengue fever represents an **emerging and under-recognized public health threat** in the United States. While global research has extensively documented its burden in endemic regions, there remains a significant gap in understanding its impact on the U.S. healthcare system — particularly in terms of hospitalization outcomes, healthcare utilization, and the vulnerability of high-risk populations.

As environmental factors (climate change, vector expansion) and societal factors (international travel, urbanization) continue to evolve, dengue is likely to pose an **increasing and preventable challenge** in the United States. A **multidisciplinary approach** integrating epidemiology, health informatics, data science, and public policy will be essential for closing evidence gaps, strengthening surveillance, and mitigating the clinical and economic burden of dengue on the U.S. population.
